Your business structure affects everything: how much you pay in taxes, whether your personal assets are protected, and how much paperwork you'll deal with.

The wrong structure can cost you thousands in unnecessary taxes ‚Äî or leave your personal savings exposed if something goes wrong.

üìä The Three Main Structures

Sole Proprietorship

"The default ‚Äî if you do nothing, this is what you are"

‚úì Pros
  • Zero setup cost or paperwork
  • Simplest tax filing (Schedule C)
  • Complete control over business
  • Can use your SSN (no EIN needed)
‚úó Cons
  • No liability protection
  • Personal assets at risk
  • Harder to get business credit
  • Less professional appearance

Limited Liability Company (LLC)

"Protection + flexibility ‚Äî the sweet spot for most small businesses"

‚úì Pros
  • Personal assets protected
  • Flexible tax options
  • Professional credibility
  • Easy to set up ($100 in Oregon)
‚úó Cons
  • $100/year Oregon renewal
  • Must keep business/personal separate
  • Still pay self-employment tax
  • Some extra record-keeping

S-Corporation

"Tax savings for profitable businesses ‚Äî but more complexity"

‚úì Pros
  • Save on self-employment taxes
  • Liability protection
  • Professional credibility
  • Can be an LLC taxed as S-Corp
‚úó Cons
  • Must run payroll (even for yourself)
  • Reasonable salary requirement
  • More expensive accounting
  • Quarterly tax filings

üíº Pro Tips From the Field

  • The "$50K rule" for S-Corp: Most accountants say S-Corp tax savings don't outweigh the costs until you're profiting $50-80K+. Below that, stick with LLC.
  • LLC ‚Üí S-Corp is easy: You can form an LLC now and elect S-Corp tax treatment later when income justifies it. Best of both worlds.
  • Liability protection isn't automatic: You must keep business and personal finances separate, or courts can "pierce the corporate veil."
  • Get an EIN regardless: Even sole props should get a free EIN from the IRS. It protects your SSN and looks more professional.
  • Talk to a CPA before S-Corp: The "reasonable salary" requirement is tricky. Too low and you'll get audited. A good CPA pays for themselves.

üéØ Quick Decision Guide

Choose Sole Proprietorship if:

You're testing a business idea, making under $20K, have low liability risk, and want zero hassle.

Choose LLC if:

You want liability protection, plan to grow, work with clients who expect it, or have any meaningful risk.

Choose S-Corp (or LLC taxed as S-Corp) if:

You're consistently profiting $60K+, want to save on self-employment taxes, and can handle payroll complexity.
